Fiction and poetry

James Mulhern is the author of two literary novels — Give Them Unquiet Dreams (2019), a Kirkus Reviews Best Book of 2019 and starred-review title, and Molly Bonamici (2016), which Kirkus Reviews praised for its “gleefully and wonderfully odd protagonist.” His work moves between Irish-Catholic Boston and Italian-American Revere, between the comic and the gothic, and between this world and what his narrators call “the second sight.”

His writing has appeared in literary journals more than two hundred times. He was nominated for a Pushcart Prize in 2017, longlisted for the Fish Short Story Prize, and shortlisted for the Aesthetica Creative Writing Award in 2021 for his poetry. He was awarded a fully funded writing fellowship to the University of Oxford. His novella A Prayer for Home (2016) was praised by The Missouri Review; his story collection Assumptions and Other Stories followed.

Mulhern brings to his fiction more than thirty-five years in publishing and education. He has served as a senior editor at Houghton Mifflin in Boston and at Clark Boardman Callaghan in New York, as Department Chair of English, as a College Board AP English consultant, and as an AP consultant for the National Math and Science Initiative. He is the founder of Silver Current Press and the 2601 Literary Salon.

He lives in Philadelphia, where he writes, teaches, and edits manuscripts.

Synopsis

Set in 1970s Irish-Catholic Boston, Give Them Unquiet Dreams follows fourteen-year-old Aiden Glencar, a gay boy with the “second sight,” as he tries to free his mother from a psychiatric institute, navigates his domineering and occasionally kleptomaniacal grandmother, and confers nightly with the spirit of his dead grandfather. A lyric, comic, and gothic family novel in the lineage of James Joyce’s The Dead and Yeats’s The Stolen Child — about grief, faith, queerness, and the porousness of past, present, and future.

Featured Title · Novel · 2016

Molly Bonamici

Silver Current Press · 258 pages · ISBN 978-1-5238-0780-2

Kirkus Reviews

“A gleefully and wonderfully odd protagonist eases readers into a bare-bones plot.”

Synopsis

Set in 1980 Revere, Massachusetts, Molly Bonamici follows a precocious, deadpan, atheist teenage girl through an Italian-American working-class family in which her beloved grandmother Nonna is gradually revealed to be a quiet serial murderer. A literary novel in the lineage of Flannery O’Connor’s grandmothers, Shirley Jackson’s We Have Always Lived in the Castle, and Elena Ferrante’s Neapolitan family novels — structured around Dante’s Inferno, an Emily Dickinson frame, and an inversion of Chopin’s The Awakening.
